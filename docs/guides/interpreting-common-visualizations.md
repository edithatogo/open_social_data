# Interpreting Common Visualizations

Charts can make social data easier to understand, but they can also hide definitions, uncertainty, or scale choices. Read the chart title, axis labels, units, source notes, and filters before interpreting a pattern.

## Line Charts

Line charts are best for change over time. Check whether the data is monthly, quarterly, annual, or irregular. Avoid treating one period as a trend. For survey results, check whether confidence intervals overlap before describing a change as meaningful.

## Bar Charts

Bar charts are useful for comparing categories at one point in time. Confirm that each bar uses the same unit and denominator. For ranked bar charts, small differences near the middle of the ranking may not be meaningful.

## Maps

Maps show geography, but large land areas can visually dominate even when few people live there. Use rates or percentages for comparisons across areas, and check whether small-area values are suppressed or unstable.

## Stacked Charts

Stacked bars or areas show composition. They are good for seeing totals and broad shares, but individual middle segments can be hard to compare. Use a table or grouped bar chart when exact category comparison matters.

## Scatter Plots

Scatter plots show relationships between two measures. They do not prove causation. Look for outliers, sample size, and whether both measures use comparable time periods and populations.

## Good Chart Habits

Keep source notes close to the chart. State whether values are counts, percentages, rates, indexes, or dollars. Use plain titles that say what the chart shows, not what the reader must conclude.
