# Ethical Use of Social Data

Open social data can support better decisions, but it can also harm people if it is used without context. Ethical use means preserving source caveats, respecting privacy protections, and being clear about uncertainty.

## Keep Privacy Protections Intact

Do not try to reverse suppression, rounding, or perturbation applied by a source agency. If a value is suppressed or flagged, keep that status visible in analysis and downstream outputs.

## Avoid Overclaiming

Most public social datasets are descriptive. They can show patterns, but they rarely prove why a pattern exists on their own. Avoid causal claims unless the method supports them.

## Use Respectful Group Labels

Use official source labels when reporting categories, but recognise that labels can change and may not capture how communities describe themselves. Avoid deficit framing when comparing groups.

## Explain Limitations

Every chart, table, or dashboard should preserve important caveats: source, date, geography, population, unit, suppression flags, and known method changes. If the data cannot answer a question, say so.

## Minimise Harm in Small Populations

Small communities and small subgroups can be identifiable or misrepresented even in aggregate data. Be careful with maps, rankings, and outlier callouts for small populations.

## Cite Sources

Credit the original agency and link to the source release or API. When using repository scripts or derived metadata, cite this project separately from the official data source.
