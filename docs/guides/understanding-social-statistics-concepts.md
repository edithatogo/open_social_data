# Understanding Social Statistics Concepts

Social statistics describe people, households, communities, services, and economic conditions. They are useful because they turn repeated observations into evidence that can be compared over time and across groups.

## Counts, Rates, Percentages, and Indexes

Counts tell you how many observations were recorded. Rates and percentages describe a value relative to a population or denominator. Indexes show relative change from a base period rather than a direct count or dollar amount.

Do not compare values until you know the unit. A count of people, a percentage of households, and an index value can all appear in the same source release but answer different questions.

## Population and Denominators

Many indicators need a denominator. For example, hospital admissions per 1,000 people require both admissions data and a population estimate. If the denominator changes, the rate can change even when the count does not.

Use the same geography, time period, and population scope when combining datasets.

## Time Periods and Revisions

Social datasets can be monthly, quarterly, annual, or survey-year based. Some values are revised after first publication. Always record the source version or access date when analysis must be reproducible.

## Survey Uncertainty

Survey results are estimates from sampled respondents. Smaller groups often have wider uncertainty and may be suppressed, rounded, or flagged. Treat source quality flags as part of the data, not as decoration.

## Comparing Groups

Group comparisons need context. Differences can reflect age structure, geography, survey design, definitions, or data quality as well as the underlying social issue. Avoid ranking small groups without checking uncertainty and caveats.
