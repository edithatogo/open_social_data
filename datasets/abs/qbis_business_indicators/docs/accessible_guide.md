# Accessible Guide to Business Indicators, Australia (QBIS)

**Understanding Australian Business Performance in Plain Language**

**Source:** Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) - Quarterly Business Indicators Survey
**Guide Updated:** $(date +"%Y-%m-%d")

---

## 1. What is This Data About?

*   **In simple terms, what does this dataset tell us?**
    This dataset gives us a regular check-up on how different parts of Australia's private businesses are doing. It looks at things like how much businesses are selling, how much they are paying in wages, what their profits look like, and how much stock (inventories) they have on hand. It's like a report card for businesses, published every three months (quarterly).
*   **Who collects this information and why?**
    The Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) collects this information. They are Australia's national statistics office. They do this to help government, businesses, and the public understand what's happening in the economy, make informed decisions, and plan for the future.
*   **What time period does it cover?**
    It includes information for many years, updated quarterly. You can usually find data going back quite a way, allowing you to see trends over time.
*   **How often is it updated?**
    Every quarter (every three months).

---

## 2. Why is This Data Important?

This data helps us understand the health of the Australian economy by looking at how businesses are performing.

*   **What kind of questions can this data help answer?**
    *   Are businesses generally making more or less profit than before?
    *   Are businesses paying out more in wages and salaries?
    *   Are businesses selling more goods and services?
    *   Are businesses building up their stocks of goods, or selling them off?
    *   Which industries are growing or shrinking?
    *   How does business activity in my state compare to the national average?
*   **Who might find this data useful?**
    *   **Government:** To make policy decisions about the economy.
    *   **Businesses:** To understand their own performance in context, and to see trends in their industry or state.
    *   **Economists and Researchers:** To analyze economic trends and make forecasts.
    *   **Students:** Learning about the Australian economy.
    *   **Anyone interested in how the economy is tracking.**

## 3. How Can You Use This Data? (Simple Examples)

*   **Example 1: Looking at Company Profits**
    *   Imagine the data shows that "Company Gross Operating Profits" for the retail industry went up by 5% in the last quarter compared to the previous quarter. This might suggest that, overall, retail businesses made more profit in that period.
*   **Example 2: Comparing Wages in Different Industries**
    *   You could look at "Wages and Salaries" for the "Manufacturing" industry and compare it to the "Mining" industry for the same quarter. This would give you an idea of the total wage bill for each of those sectors. (Note: this is the total amount, not average wages per person).

## 4. Key Things to Know Before Using This Data

*   **What does it *not* tell you directly?**
    *   It doesn't tell you about government or non-profit organizations (it's focused on private sector businesses).
    *   It doesn't usually tell you about *individual* business performance, but rather about whole industries or the total economy.
    *   It doesn't tell you the *number* of businesses, but rather their economic activity (sales, profits, etc.).
*   **Original vs. Seasonally Adjusted vs. Trend Data:**
    *   The data often comes in a few forms:
        *   **Original:** The raw numbers as collected. These can jump around due to normal seasonal changes (e.g., retail sales are often higher before Christmas).
        *   **Seasonally Adjusted:** Numbers that have had these predictable seasonal patterns removed. This makes it easier to see underlying changes.
        *   **Trend:** A further smoothed version of seasonally adjusted data to show the longer-term direction.
    *   It's important to know which version you are looking at. For seeing underlying economic movements, "Seasonally Adjusted" or "Trend" data is often preferred.
*   **Current Prices vs. Chain Volume Measures:**
    *   **Current Prices:** Values are in the dollars of the day, so they include the effect of inflation.
    *   **Chain Volume Measures:** These are adjusted to remove the effects of price changes (inflation). They help you see if the actual *volume* or *quantity* of goods and services has changed.
*   **Revisions:** The ABS sometimes updates (revises) past data as more complete information becomes available. Always try to use the latest available figures.
*   **Definitions Matter:** How the ABS defines "profit" or "wages" might be specific. Check the detailed notes in the `README.md` or the official `data_dictionary.md` (when fully populated) or ABS website.
