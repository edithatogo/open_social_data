# Accessible Guide to MyHospitals - Admissions Data (MYH-ADM)

**Understanding Australian Hospital Admissions in Plain Language**

**Source:** Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW) - MyHospitals (`MYH-ADM` measure category)
**Guide Updated:** 2025-07-08; reviewed 2026-06-18.

---

## 1. What is This Data About?

*   **In simple terms, what does this dataset tell us?**
    This dataset counts the number of times patients are formally admitted to hospitals in Australia. It can tell us how many admissions there were for different types of care (like medical emergencies, surgery, maternity care, or mental health care) at various public and private hospitals.
*   **Who collects this information and why?**
    The Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW) collects and reports this on their MyHospitals website. This information helps to understand hospital activity levels, plan for future healthcare needs, and see how different hospitals are being used.
*   **What time period does it cover?**
    The data usually covers financial years (e.g., "2011–12", "2022-23") and is updated by AIHW over time.
*   **How often is it updated?**
    AIHW updates MyHospitals data periodically, often annually for many types of data. For the most current information, it's best to check the AIHW website.

---

## 2. Why is This Data Important?

Hospital admissions data gives us a picture of how busy hospitals are and the types of care people need most.

*   **What kind of questions can this data help answer?**
    *   How many medical emergency admissions did "Hospital X" have last year?
    *   Are surgical admissions increasing or decreasing at a particular hospital or in a state?
    *   Which hospitals handle the most maternity admissions?
    *   How does the number of mental health admissions compare across different types of hospitals?
*   **Who might find this data useful?**
    *   **Health Planners & Policymakers:** To understand demand for hospital services and allocate resources.
    *   **Hospitals:** To track their own activity levels and compare with similar hospitals.
    *   **Researchers:** To study trends in healthcare usage and outcomes.
    *   **The Public:** To get a sense of the scale of hospital activity in their area or across the country.

## 3. How Can You Use This Data? (Simple Examples)

*   **Example 1: Comparing Admissions by Type of Care**
    *   You could look at "Hospital A" for the 2022-23 financial year. The data might show:
        *   Medical (emergency) admissions: 5,000
        *   Surgical admissions: 3,000
        *   Maternity admissions: 1,000
    *   This tells you that for Hospital A in that year, medical emergency admissions were the most common among these categories.
*   **Example 2: Looking at Trends Over Time (if multiple periods are available)**
    *   If you had data for "Hospital B" for "Surgical admissions" for three years:
        *   2020-21: 2,500 admissions
        *   2021-22: 2,600 admissions
        *   2022-23: 2,750 admissions
    *   This suggests a gradual increase in surgical admissions at Hospital B over those years.

## 4. Key Things to Know Before Using This Data

*   **"Admissions" vs. "Patients":** An admission is an episode of care. One person might be admitted multiple times in a year, so the number of admissions is usually higher than the number of unique patients.
*   **Type of Care:** The data is often broken down by "type of care" (e.g., medical, surgical, maternity, mental health, rehabilitation). It's important to look at the specific type you're interested in.
*   **Public vs. Private Hospitals:** The data includes both, and their admission patterns can be different.
*   **Hospital Peer Groups:** AIHW often groups hospitals by size and type (e.g., "Major hospitals," "Children's hospitals"). Comparing hospitals within the same peer group can be more meaningful.
*   **Data Definitions:** AIHW has specific definitions for what counts as an "admission" and for each "type of care." These definitions are important for accurate understanding.
*   **Caveats and Data Quality:** Sometimes, data for a particular hospital or period might have notes (caveats) attached due to data quality issues or other special circumstances. Always check for these. "N.P." often means "Not Published" and can occur for various reasons, including data quality or confidentiality.
